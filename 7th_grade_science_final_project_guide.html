Scratch Science Simulation
Animate a scientific process with moving parts + interactive text
What You're Making

Create an animated, interactive Scratch project that shows how a real scientific process works. This is NOT a slideshow. Molecules, cells, organelles, or structures must actually move on screen. Your project must also teach the viewer what's happening through on-screen text or spoken narration.

Choose Your Topic
🧬Cell Division (Mitosis or Meiosis)
🌿Photosynthesis
⚡Cellular Respiration
⚖️Finding Density
💡Your own idea (ask teacher first!)
Requirements — All 8 Must Be Met
  • 1
    Real animation — not slides. At least 3 sprites or parts must visibly move, change, grow, split, or react on screen. Clicking "next" to show a new background does not count as animation.
  • 2
    Correct science. The process you animate must match what actually happens in nature. Labels, names, and steps must be accurate.
  • 3
    On-screen text that explains. Every major step in the process must have a clear, readable text explanation that appears during that step — like a caption or narration box.
  • 4
    At least 3 labeled stages or steps. Example for mitosis: show Interphase → Prophase → Metaphase → Anaphase → Telophase, with each stage named on screen.
  • 5
    Interactive start. The project should not auto-play everything. Use a "Start" button or a key press so the viewer is in control.
  • 6
    Original sprites and backgrounds. Draw your own sprites in the Scratch editor, or use built-in Scratch assets. Do not import artwork you did not create.
  • 7
    A title screen. First thing the viewer sees is your project name and your name.
  • 8
    Share the link. Submit your project as a Scratch share link. Make sure it is set to "Share" — not just saved. Test the link before submitting.

🧠 How to make it feel alive, not like a slideshow

  • Use the glide block to move molecules from one place to another
  • Use costume changes to show a cell growing or changing shape
  • Use wait blocks so text appears as the animation plays, not all at once
  • Use show / hide to make new structures appear at the right moment
  • Try cloning to duplicate molecules or cells multiplying
🎵
micro:bit Jukebox
Code two playable songs on your micro:bit using MakeCode
What You're Making

Program your micro:bit to be a mini jukebox with two different songs or tunes. Each song is triggered by a different button or gesture. The LED display should show something while the song plays. This project uses MakeCode (blocks or JavaScript — your choice).

Requirements — All 6 Must Be Met
  • 1
    Two distinct songs/tunes. Each must have at least 8 notes. They should sound clearly different from each other — not just the same melody played twice.
  • 2
    Separate triggers. Song 1 plays when Button A is pressed. Song 2 plays when Button B is pressed. (You may use shake or tilt as a third trigger for a bonus feature.)
  • 3
    LED display feedback. While each song plays, the LED screen shows something — a scrolling song name, a dancing pattern, a simple picture, or an animation. No blank screen.
  • 4
    Songs must be coded note-by-note. Do not use the built-in "play melody" preset melodies (like Dadadum or Entertainer). Code each note yourself using the play tone block or equivalent.
  • 5
    A welcome screen on startup. When the micro:bit turns on, the LED display scrolls your name or a title like "🎵 Jukebox" before waiting for input.
  • 6
    Submit your code. Download and submit your .hex file AND a screenshot or photo of your MakeCode blocks. Write a 2–3 sentence explanation of how your jukebox works and what songs you chose.

🎹 Tips for writing your songs

  • Look up the note frequency chart — Middle C = 262 Hz, D = 294 Hz, E = 330 Hz, and so on
  • Short notes (1 beat) + long notes (2 beats) make melodies sound more interesting
  • Add a rest (silence) between phrases to make it sound musical, not rushed
  • Try to code a song you know — Happy Birthday, Hot Cross Buns, or a video game tune you like
  • Test on a real micro:bit with a speaker or headphone adapter — the simulator doesn't always sound the same

How to Search for Ideas (the right way)
  • ✓
    Look up how the science works — search "how does photosynthesis work" or "mitosis steps explained" to understand what you're animating.
  • ✓
    Watch tutorials for Scratch techniques — search "how to clone a sprite in Scratch" or "how to use glide block." Learn the technique, then apply it yourself.
  • ✓
    Look up note frequencies for micro:bit — search "note frequency chart Hz" to find the right numbers for your song notes.

    Do NOT open someone else's Scratch project, click "Remix," and submit it. Do NOT copy code from a GitHub repo or YouTube tutorial and submit it as your own.
