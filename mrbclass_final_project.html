Project 1 — Scratch Science Simulation

Animate a scientific process with moving parts + explanatory text
Create an animated, interactive Scratch project that shows how a real scientific process works. This is NOT a slideshow. Molecules, cells, organelles, or structures must actually move on screen. Your project must also teach the viewer what is happening through on-screen text or narration that appears during the animation.
Choose Your Topic
🧬 Cell Division (Mitosis / Meiosis) 🌿 Photosynthesis ⚡ Cellular Respiration ⚖️ Finding Density 💡 Your own idea — ask Mr. B first!
Requirements — All 8 Must Be Met
  • 1
    Real animation — not slides. At least 3 sprites or parts must visibly move, change, grow, split, or react on screen. Clicking "next" to show a new background does not count as animation.
  • 2
    Correct science. The process you animate must match what actually happens in nature. Labels, names, and steps must be accurate.
  • 3
    On-screen text that explains. Every major step must have a clear, readable text explanation that appears during that step — like a caption or narration box.
  • 4
    At least 3 labeled stages or steps. Example for mitosis: Interphase → Prophase → Metaphase → Anaphase → Telophase, with each stage named on screen.
  • 5
    Interactive start. Use a "Start" button or a key press — don't auto-play everything at once.
  • 6
    Original sprites and backgrounds. Draw your own sprites in the Scratch editor or use built-in Scratch assets. Do not import artwork you did not create.
  • 7
    A title screen. First thing the viewer sees is your project name and your name.
  • 8
    Share the link. Submit as a Scratch share link. Set it to "Share" — not just saved. Test the link before submitting.

🧠 How to make it feel alive — not like a slideshow

  • Use the glide block to move molecules from one place to another
  • Use costume changes to show a cell growing or changing shape
  • Use wait blocks so text appears as the animation plays, not all at once
  • Use show / hide to make new structures appear at the right moment
  • Try cloning to duplicate molecules or cells that are multiplying

Project 2 — micro:bit Jukebox

Code two playable songs triggered by different buttons
Program your micro:bit to be a mini jukebox with two different songs or tunes, each triggered by a different button. The LED display must show something while each song plays. Use MakeCode at makecode.microbit.org — blocks or JavaScript, your choice.
🎹 How the buttons work:
Your jukebox must respond to button presses like this:
A Song 1 plays
B Song 2 plays
A+B Bonus: 3rd feature (optional)
When Button A is pressed → Song 1 starts playing and the LED shows its display.
When Button B is pressed → Song 2 starts playing and the LED shows a different display.
The two songs must sound clearly different from each other — not the same melody repeated.
For a bonus, pressing A+B together can trigger a third tune, animation, or special feature.
Requirements — All 6 Must Be Met
  • 1
    Two distinct songs/tunes. Each must have at least 8 notes. They should sound clearly different from each other — not the same melody played twice at different speeds.
  • 2
    Button A → Song 1. Button B → Song 2. Each button reliably starts the correct song. Songs should not overlap — if a song is playing, pressing the other button should switch to the new song.
  • 3
    LED display feedback. While each song plays, the LED shows something — a scrolling song name, a dancing pattern, a simple picture, or an animation. No blank screen.
  • 4
    Songs must be coded note-by-note. Do not use built-in preset melodies (like Dadadum or Entertainer). Code each note yourself using the play tone block or equivalent.
  • 5
    A welcome screen on startup. When the micro:bit turns on, the LED scrolls your name or a title (like "🎵 Jukebox") before waiting for a button press.
  • 6
    Submit your code. Download and submit your .hex file AND a screenshot or photo of your MakeCode blocks. Write 2–3 sentences explaining how your jukebox works and what songs you chose.

🎹 Tips for writing your songs note-by-note

  • Look up the note frequency chart: Middle C = 262 Hz, D = 294 Hz, E = 330 Hz, F = 349 Hz, G = 392 Hz
  • Mix short notes (1 beat) and long notes (2 beats) to make melodies sound more musical
  • Add a rest (silence) between phrases so the song breathes — don't rush it
  • Try coding a song you already know: Happy Birthday, Hot Cross Buns, a video game tune
  • Test on a real micro:bit with a speaker or headphone adapter — the simulator can sound different
  • Make your two songs feel different in mood: one fast and fun, one slow and calm
